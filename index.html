Course Breakdown

During the three year course, you will work on individual and group projects for each of the below units:

Year 1: Participatory Cultures (30 Credits) Term 1 - The Culture of the Web. During the first term of the unit, you will participate in group online exploration and critique, as well as a group project. You will gain a basic understanding of navigation, purpose, social systems, business models, and users. There will be case studies on social media such as chat, instant messaging, Facebook and Twitter, as well as online games.

What you need to do: During this term, you will need to work collaboratively to design a shared online environment and pitch this to the unit leader (James Morris). Then, individually you will need to reflect on your performance and what elements you brought to the project.

Term 2 - Building and Managing a Participatory Space In the second term, the focus narrows onto Users and user generated content. In this half of the unit, you will learn the skills you need to create and manage a social space for active users, such as online forums, user generated content sites and social media. You will also participate in a moderation test, to learn about the different roles at play in online forums and you will build your own online social community as a test scenario.

What you need to do: You will be expected to participate in discussions during lectures and group workshops, and to present your own ideas and examples during workshops. You will also have to carry out your own research by participating in various online forums, social networks and user-generated content sites. In groups, you will build and manage an online community.

Fundamentals of Web Design (30 Credits) Theme 1 - Magazine Mash up This part of the unit will focus on building content-focused magazine websites for specific audiences. You will learn about the history of the web, and how magazine websites have grown in popularity since the invention of the web.

What you need to do: Ravensbourne is host to the annual MozFest, held by Mozilla and each year, every Web Media student is given a free ticket to the weekend of talks and workshops hosted by various tech companies. For this unit, you will need to report on the goings on at the festival in groups. You will build a website to post blogs, videos and images and interact with the other attendees through social media. For the second part of the unit, you will need to build another website for blog posts, videos and images for the TeenTech event. TeenTech is an event held in the Copperbox arena, Stratford and is for young people aged 11-16 to get them interested in jobs in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics). Your grade will depend on the quality of work you produce and how well you work as a team.

Fundamentals of Web Design (30 Credits) Theme 2 - Web Design Principles In this project theme you will learn the principles of visual communication by researching and designing objects that communicate. In particular, you will learn how people make sense of visual perceptions, how people get information from the web (how they read the page), how to look at everyday objects in a way to make them appear to be something else. You will also learn the basics of HTML and CSS.

What you need to do: There are multiple mini-projects to complete for this part of the unit: Eyebombing: Finding faces in everyday places and photographing them TheUnionHack: Mashing up flags to create new ones, and designing your own flag OneTweet: Designing a vector poster of a famous tweet to learn about visual design principles CodeInYourPlate: Coding your favourite recipe into a webpage.

The Web Industry (15 Credits) During this unit, you will choose an area within the web industry to conduct a research project. You will need to conduct primary and secondary research and produce a written report of 1000-1500 words in your choice of medium (web page or essay). You will need to use the harvard referencing system.

Web Development Workshop (30 Credits) During this unit, you will gain a deeper knowledge of HTML and CSS and a little bit of Javascript. You will also learn about the importance of content strategies and targeted audiences.

What you need to do: There are two mini projects that make up this unit, the details of both are below: Sharing-Is-Caring: For this project, you need to pick a cause you care about and create a one-page website advocating it, using the visual design techniques and audience targeting you learned about in the first term. Our-Space: For this group project, you will need to create a website for the Web Media course to entice prospective students (can you what unit we’re doing?)

Year 2: Creative Collaboration (30 Credits) This unit focuses on a variety of e-commerce platforms and gives students the opportunity to develop their entrepreneurial skills. Throughout its duration, Web Media students will collaborate with those from Digital Photography to develop e-commerce sites to market & sell photographic images.

The Dynamic Web (15 Credits) During this unit you will learn how to mash-up Web technologies to make tools that everyone can use. By the end of this project, students will have become more familiar with programming, coding and research.

Designing the User Experience (30 Credits) This unit introduces students to the methods and tools used to effectively address the needs and circumstances of Web users. You will learn to design and prototype Web products that are comfortable and even joyful to use.

Interactive Narratives (30 Credits) Interactive Narratives will give students the chance to tell a story across different platforms, online games in particular. In the first half of this unit, students will create a narrative, or rework an existing one, as an interactive experience, which will also serve as the main “backstory” for the online game. In the second half, this backstory then serve as the foundations for the creation of a game or series of mini games.

Year 3: Advanced Web Technologies (30 Credits) This unit focuses on new and advancing web technologies, focusing on big data and audio-visual enhancements. What you need to do:

In small groups, you will produce a pitch for a new service or adaptation of an existing website, blog or social media area to incorporate ‘intelligent’ content for commercial or social benefit. This will illustrate a personal understanding of the range of tools and techniques which offer some level of ‘intelligence’ to users and producers, and could include RSS feeds, tracking, monitoring of user behaviours, intelligent search, conversational agents or user interfaces, tag clouds, integration with iPhone applications, micro-payments, and so on.

Digital Advertising (15 Credits) In this unit, you will produce and pitch an advertising/marketing campaign for your portfolio that showcases your ideas, intelligence, communication skills and web media skills. Think and work to impress a potential employer!

Major Project (45 Credits) The Major Project is the primary portfolio work a student performs to showcase their talent for the world of employment. This is where you show the world what you can do – or even create the foundations of the startup company that you will build into a successful business after graduation.

Students have the choice of either leading their own practical project OR producing a detailed business plan for a business start-up OR specializing in a particular skill area of Web Media.

100% of your work at Web Media is graded by coursework. No stressful all-nighters spent studying before a big exam!

Uni life 

House Rules

Like most courses and universities, RaveWebMedia has a set of house rules that students are expected to comply to, so we can all learn in a happy and collaborative environment.

  • Be Present.If you happen to be late (even by 5 minutes) or absent, make sure you email your lecturer about it before a session starts.
  • Participatein class debates and workshops. We'll make sure that your ideas have space to be heard and that nobody makes you feel uncomfortable about sharing them.
  • Presentyour work during mock and final assessments. If you can't make it those days then you'll record your presentation and upload it to YouTube (or similar).
  • Be a designer.From the very beginning we’ll treat you like designers and web developers, not students.You will be responsible for your own ideas, work and dealines, just like you will be in your chosen career path.

lifestyle

All set in the heart of London, next to the world’s busiest entertainment venue, The O2 Arena, Ravensbourne has everything you’ll need. Right next door you’ll find a cinema, bowling alley and a wide variety of restaurants and bars.

There are lots of accomodation options for students that live outside of London. Prices are typically between £9,581 and £10,647. Scape is a popular halls company, as is Unite

The nearest tube station to Ravensbourne is North Greenwich, which is in Oyster card zones 2/3, with a student oyster card, you can get a 1/3 off the fare. There are also options to get the bus, DLR or Thames Clipper to and from uni.

industry links 

The course has strong industry ties, giving students frequent opportunities for work on live industry web projects, internships, and frequent professional guest lectures from the likes of Google, Mind Candy and Mozilla, amongst others.
Ravensbourne has its own work agency for current and past students, called the Ravensbourne Agency. Lots of companies within the industry have profiles and look to the Ravensbourne agency to find interns and work placements.

Entry requirements

You are expected to have five GCSEs (grade C or above) or equivalent (including English and Mathematics) and also to have at least one of the following or equivalent UK or international qualification:
- 2 A levels, grade C or above
- 4 AS levels, grade C or above
- 2 vocational A level, grade C or above
- Level 3 Foundation Diploma or National Diploma
- Advanced Diploma, grade C or above
- International Baccalaureate, 28 points or above
